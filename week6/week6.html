Week 6: The connection between statistical mechanics and ensemble forecast I: Applications¶

Connections between Liouville equation and ensemble forecasts¶

Last week, we learned the physical meaning of Jacobian matrix, determinant and Liouville equation. This week, we will walk through a few of their applications. Before we introduce the examples, we need to know there are two equivalent formulas of Liouville equation: (1) initial state-based Liouville equation and (2) current state-based Liouville equation. One easy way to distinguish these two is looking at how (44) is defined.

First, let’s consider a dynamical system only with first-order derivative.

(50)¶\[\begin{align} \mathbf{\dot{X}} = \mathbf{\Phi}(\mathbf{X},t) \end{align}\]

In the initial state-based method, the Liouville equation is only a function of initial state and time. Physically, we can know how a dynamical system evolves with time as long as we know its initial states and time for integration. i.e.,

(51)¶\[\begin{align} \mathbf{X} = \mathbf{X}(\mathbf{\Xi},t) \end{align}\]

where \(\Xi\) is the initial state and \(t\) represent the number of time steps we integrate forward. Then the determinant of the Jacobian matrix, which exerts on the initial condition can be written as

(52)¶\[\rho (\mathbf{\Xi},t) = \mathrm{det}(\frac{\partial \mathbf{X}}{\partial \mathbf{\Xi}})\]

While the dynamical system is deterministic, we can also find an 1-on-1 bijection between \(\mathbf{\Xi}\) and \(\mathbf{X}\) at a given t. i.e.,

(53)¶\[\begin{align} \mathbf{\Xi} = \mathbf{\Xi}(\mathbf{X},t) \end{align}\]

The existence of 1-on-1 bijection enables us to rewrite (53) i.e., represent \(\mathrm{\Xi}\) with \(\mathrm{X}\)

(54)¶\[(\frac{\partial }{\partial t} + \sum_{i}\Phi_i(\mathbf{X},t)\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{X}})\rho = \rho(\mathrm{X},t)\frac{\partial \mathbf{\Phi}(\mathbf{X},t)}{\partial \mathbf{X}}\]

compare (54) to the Liouville equation as a function of initial state (similar to (47))

(55)¶\[\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} = \rho(\mathrm{\Xi},t)\frac{\partial \mathbf{\Phi}(\mathbf{X},t)}{\partial \mathbf{X}}\vert_{\mathbf{X(\mathbf{\Xi})}}\]

By observing (54) and (55), it’s not hard to find their analogs in equations of fluid dynamics. (54) is similar to so-called Eluerian form (i.e., observing the system at a fixed point), while (55) is similar to Lagrangian form (i.e, observers following the particle). This is also the reason why it’s called “particle flow”.

Liouville equation in 1st-order ODE case¶

To illustrative the mathematical equivalence between (54) and (55), we use a Riccati equation (i.e., equation with form of \(x^{'}=P(t)x^2+Q(t)x+R(t)\)) to demonstrate

(56)¶\[\dot{X} = -X^2\]

the analytical solution is

(57)¶\[X = \frac{\Xi}{1+\Xi t}\]

where \(\Xi\) is the initial condition and \(t\) is time of integration. According to (52), we can find the initial state as a function of current state.

(58)¶\[\Xi = \frac{X}{1-X t}\]

The Jacobian as a function of initial state can be written as,

(59)¶\[\rho(\Xi,t) = \frac{\partial X}{\partial \Xi} = \frac{1}{(1+t\Xi)^{2}} \]

and the Jacobian as a function of current state can be written as,

(60)¶\[\rho(X,t) = \frac{\partial X}{\partial \Xi} = \frac{1}{(1+t\frac{X}{1-X t})^{2}} = (1-tX)^2 \]
